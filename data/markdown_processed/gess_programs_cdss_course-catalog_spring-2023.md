## 🔬 Advanced Quantitative Methods & Design

### Advanced Quantitative Methods (Political Science)

**Prerequisites:** Knowledge of Multivariate Analysis
**Course Content:** The goal of this course is to provide an introduction into maximum-likelihood estimation. Students who wish to pass this course must complete homework assignments and produce a research paper.

- **Literature:** Eliason, Scott R. (1993). *Maximum Likelihood Estimation: Logic and Practice*. Newbury Park: Sage. Long, J. Scott. (1997). *Regression Models for Categorical and Limited Dependent Variables*. Newbury Park: Sage. King, Gary. (2008). *Unifying political methodology: the likelihood theory of statistical inference*. Ann Arbor, MI: University of Michigan Press.
- **Assessment:** Homework assignments, research paper (all graded).
- **Tutorial:** The tutorial accompanies the course “Advanced Quantitative Methods” in Political Science. Lab sessions focus on practical issues like data preparation, statistical software usage, appropriate tests, and result interpretation. No prior knowledge of statistical programming is expected.

### Causal Inference in Social Science

**Prerequisites:** Active participation, term paper (graded)
**Course Content:** Questions of cause and effect are central to social science. This course introduces key conceptual and methodological approaches to tackle causal inference, including:

- The potential outcomes model of causal inference.
- Experimental designs.
- Matching and regression.
- Instrumental variables, regression discontinuity designs, and difference-in-differences and fixed effects.
- **Assessment:** Active participation, term paper (graded).
- **Schedule:**
  - **Workshop:**
    - biweekly: 17.02.23 – 31.03.23 | Friday | 13:45 – 17:00 | 211 in B6, 30–32
    - biweekly: 21.04.23 – 02.06.23 | Friday | 13:45 – 17:00 | 211 in B6, 30–32

### Experimental Research Designs

**Course Content:** Experimental designs are crucial for causal identification. This seminar provides a broad overview of experimental methods (survey, field, lab-in-the-field, and laboratory experiments). Students discuss classical and recent work, covering best practices like transparency (open science) and ethical considerations. The goal is for students to critically think about and design their own experiments.
**Assessment:** Weekly preparation of two discussion-questions, one presentation, active participation, presentation of the Exposé of the seminar paper (graded), and a research design seminar paper (graded).
**Schedule:**

- **Seminar:**
  - biweekly: 18.04.23 – 30.05.23 | Tuesday | 08:30 – 11:45 | C 116 in A5, 6 entrance C | [Link](link)

### Survey Design

**Course Content:** Surveys are a major data source for quantitative social science research. This graduate-level course teaches the fundamentals of survey design, covering the major steps of implementation and conducting a survey, and discussing sources of error at each step. The course uses well-known large-scale surveys (e.g., ALLBUS, ESS, EVS, SOEP) for illustration.
**Assessment:** Active participation, homework assignments/oral presentations, term paper (graded).
**Schedule:**

- **Seminar:**
  - 16.02.23 – 01.06.23 | Thursday | 13:45 – 15:15 | tbc

### Longitudinal Data Analysis

**Prerequisites:** Some basic knowledge of statistical inference and R is required
**Course Content:** Provides a broad overview of methods for longitudinal data analysis, focusing on panel data. It discusses the advantages of panel data, main models (pooled OLS, fixed effects, random effects, first-differences), panel generalized linear models, and event history analysis.

- **Lecture:** The course provides an overview of methods used in longitudinal data analysis.
- **Tutorial:** Using R, methods are applied to real survey data.
- **Assessment:** Lecture – Three quizzes (two must be a pass), regular attendance, written examination (graded, closed-book). Tutorial – compulsory attendance, twelve homework assignments of which nine must be passed.
- **Schedule:**

### Data Science and Machine Learning with Python

**Prerequisites:** Some programming skills (Python, R, JAVA, C, HTML, BASH, etc.) OR motivation to learn Python.
**Course Content:** This seminar covers the major steps of statistical analysis in exploratory data analysis and data science. It is divided into three parts:

1. Basics of Python and key libraries (including "programming paradigms" and "big data").
1. Data exploration, data visualization, and statistical modeling with Python.
1. Basics of machine learning (supervised, unsupervised, semi-supervised) and neural networks (covering "computer vision," "computer linguistics," and "AI").
   The course culminates in applying these skills to real-world projects.
   **Assessment:** Coding-homework, Data analysis project written in Python (including data transformation, visualization, and analysis) (graded).
   **Schedule:**

  - biweekly: 21.04.23 – 02.06.23 | Friday | 08:30 – 13:30 | tbc

### Reading Course: Sampling and Causal Inference

**Prerequisites:** Aimed at Ph.D. students in or beyond their second year. Basic statistics and prior experience with R or Stata are helpful but not necessary.
**Course Content:** This hands-on course examines the interplay between sampling and experimental methods, statistical inference, and estimation of causal effects. It moves beyond standard econometric assumptions by starting at the design stage. Participants use the German Business Panel as a case study to implement cutting-edge methods. Students present research papers and write a short technical report.
**Learning Outcomes:** Develop skills in questionnaire/survey experiment design, data analysis, quantitative methods, classification, inference, and report writing.
**Assessment:** Paper (technical report) (optional), Presentation (50 %), Class Participation (50 %).
**Schedule:**

- **Lecture:**
  - 14.02.23 – 30.05.23 | Tuesday | 10:15 – 11:45 | B 144, A5, 6 – B

## 🌐 Interdisciplinary & Big Picture Topics

### Interdisciplinary Research Methods

**Course Content:** This course aims to initiate interdisciplinary projects, fostering the interdisciplinary spirit of GESS students. The course structure includes four core building blocks:

1. **Kick-Off & Introductory Session:** Participants present an interdisciplinary paper idea or dataset.
1. **Mini-Research-Day:** Presenting current working papers and discussing peers' work.
1. **Science Speed Dating:** Short talks to match research interests and develop collaborative proposals.
1. **Project Presentations & Writeups:** Final presentation and write-up of the collaborative proposal.
   **Assessment:** Pass/fail. Requires active participation in all four building blocks, a short paper presentation, and a final group write-up.

### Climate and Sustainable Finance

**Course Content:** Introduces graduate students to questions and methods in the rapidly evolving fields of climate/sustainable finance. It connects global researchers and addresses how climate change requires changes across natural, social, and economic systems.
**Assessment:** Beyond weekly preparation and participation, students write a paper either laying out a potential research topic or synthesizing related papers.
**Schedule:**

- **Lecture:** 24.01.23 – 11.04.23 | Tuesday | 17:00 – 19:00 | online (Zoom)

### Big Questions in Economics and Politics

**Course Content:** A course exploring major questions at the intersection of economics and politics (e.g., the role of morals, digitalization, and human dignity). Students read and discuss four key books: *Capitalism and Freedom* (Friedman), *Moral Tribes* (Greene), *The Tyranny of Merit* (Sandel), and *The Age of Surveillance Capitalism* (Zuboff).
**Assessment:** Students must read the required books, form opinions, and defend views in group discussions and a final essay.

## 🧠 Specialized Social Science Fields

### Health Sociology: Social Determinants of Health

**Course Content:** Offers an introduction to the health consequences of social position and social circumstances. The course is structured in three parts:

1. Key notions of health sociology and social factors in population health.
1. Individual factors associated with health inequalities (socioeconomic status, gender, work, migration).
1. Macro determinants of health (income inequality, social capital, and policy).
   **Assessment:** Active participation, an oral presentation, and a final paper (graded, 4,000–4,500 words).
   **Schedule:**

- **Seminar:** 15.02.23 – 31.05.23 | Wednesday | 13:45 – 15:15 | A 103 in B6, 23–25

### International Political Economy (IPE)

**Course Content:** Introduces current research topics in IPE, examining how international and domestic politics interact with global flows of goods, finance, and people. Topics include international trade, global financial flows, international development, and the role of international institutions.
**Assessment:** Active participation, term paper (graded).
**Schedule:**

- **Lecture:**
  - 19.04.23 – 31.05.23 | Wednesday | 10:15 – 11:45 | C 217 in A5, 6 entrance C | [Link](link)
  - 20.04.23 – 01.06.23 | Thursday | 13:45 – 15:15 | B 243 in A5, 6 entrance B

### Social Network Analysis

**Course Content:** A general introduction to social network analysis, focusing heavily on political sociology. It treats network analysis both as a theoretical approach (viewing relations as basic building blocks) and a methodological toolkit. Key concepts include weak ties, brokerage, and diffusion. The course has a large practical component.
**Assessment:** Regular & active participation, formulation of questions/comments to literature, term paper (graded).
**Schedule:**

- **Seminar:** 13.02.23 – 22.05.23 | Monday | 13:45 – 15:15 | C 217 in A5, 6 entrance C

### Migration and Populism

**Course Content:** Addresses the measurement and explanation of xenophobic and populist attitudes in the context of increasing migration and the rise of right-wing populist parties. The seminar discusses current and innovative approaches, and replicates existing studies.
**Assessment:** Participation, weekly reading, presentation of an empirical study, term paper (graded).
**Schedule:**

- **Seminar:** 15.02.23 – 31.05.23 | Wednesday | 10:15 – 11:45 | B 317 in A5, 6 entrance B | [Link](link)

### Political Economy of Inequality

**Course Content:** Studies economic inequality from a political economy perspective. It covers concepts and measurement of inequality, general global trends, determinants (political and economic factors), and the implications of inequality for various political and economic outcomes. The methodological focus is on quantitative methods for causal inference.
**Assessment:** Active participation, term paper (graded).
**Schedule:**

- **Seminar:**
