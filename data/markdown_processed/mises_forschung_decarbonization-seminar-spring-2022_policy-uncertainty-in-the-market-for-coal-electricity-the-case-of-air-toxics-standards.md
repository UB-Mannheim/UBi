# Policy Uncertainty in the Market for Coal Electricity: The Case of Air Toxics Standards

**Speaker:** Ashley Langer
**Affiliation:** University of Arizona
**Date:** March 21, 2022
**Format:** Live virtual event (Zoom)

## Speaker Biography

Ashley Langer is an Associate Professor of Economics at the University of Arizona. Her research utilizes frontier economic methods to evaluate the impact of environmental and energy policies. Professor Langer's interest in environmental economics stems from the observation that, because individual choices have environmental repercussions, policies such as subsidies, regulations, and standards are often crucial for improving environmental outcomes. Her research evaluates how alternative policy approaches change environmental outcomes by merging theoretical insights with econometric modeling, allowing her to recover the drivers of individual and firm behavior.

*Previously, Professor Langer worked at the University of Michigan and the Brookings Institution, and earned degrees from the University of California-Berkeley and Northwestern University.*

## Seminar Abstract

Policy uncertainty significantly affects many important irreversible decisions, including investment, technology adoption, and exit. This is particularly true for environmental policy in the U.S., where Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) rule-making is subject to uncertainty due to judicial challenge and executive turnover.

In this paper, the research estimates a dynamic oligopoly model where merchant coal generators face uncertainty over whether the Mercury and Air Toxics Standard (MATS) will be enforced in the future. To estimate annual profits—a key input into the model—the authors develop new approaches to estimating ramping and operation and maintenance costs.

The study estimates generators' beliefs regarding the probability of future enforcement and simulates how policy uncertainty affects generator exit, equilibrium costs, and pollution. The findings reveal that generators subject to MATS faced substantial uncertainty regarding the policy's enforcement, with implied probabilities of enforcement of 70% in 2013 and 36% in 2014. This uncertainty led to significant delays in both exit and the adoption of abatement technologies, and increased generator compliance costs by $1.6 billion.
