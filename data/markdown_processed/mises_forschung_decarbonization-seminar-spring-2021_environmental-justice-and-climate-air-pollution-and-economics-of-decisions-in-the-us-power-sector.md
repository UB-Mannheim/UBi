# Environmental Justice, Climate, Air Pollution, and Economics of Decisions in the U.S. Power Sector

**Speaker:** Inês Azevedo (Stanford University)
**Date:** Monday, March 22, 2021
**Format:** Live virtual event (Registration closed)

## Seminar Abstract: Research Findings

The seminar addresses the complex relationship between electricity generation, air pollution, and public health, focusing on the economic and environmental justice implications within the U.S. power sector.

**Key Research Areas:**

- **Air Pollution Impact:** Electricity generation is a major contributor to PM2.5 air pollution. The study estimates health effects from this pollution across seven Regional Transmission Organizations and all US states, analyzing impacts by income and race.
- **Disparities:** While exposures are higher for lower-income populations, the research finds that racial disparities are larger than income disparities.
- **Geographical Mismatch:** A significant finding is the large difference between where electricity is generated and where the resulting health consequences are experienced. For 36 US states, most health impacts are attributable to emissions originating in other states.
- **Policy Gap:** The talk highlights that while improved air quality and human health are often discussed as "co-benefits" of mitigating climate change, they are rarely considered when designing or implementing climate policies.
- **Modeling Solutions:** The researchers developed and implemented a model that optimizes emissions reduction costs from the U.S. power sector by considering both climate and health benefits.
  - The model determines the optimal locations for replacing power plants with new wind, solar, or natural gas facilities to meet a CO2 reduction target.
  - It employs a capacity expansion model that integrates assessments of climate and health damages, comparing portfolios optimized for climate benefits alone versus those optimized for both health and climate.
- **Health Benefits Quantification:** The model estimates county-level health damages and accounts for uncertainty using multiple air quality models (AP3, EASIUR, InMAP) and concentration−response functions (American Cancer Society and Harvard Six Cities).
  - The results show that reducing CO2 by 30% yields $21–68 billion in annual health benefits.
  - Co-optimizing for both climate and health benefits can provide an additional $9–36 billion.
  - Crucially, the total health benefits are found to equal or exceed climate benefits across a wide range of modeling assumptions.

**Conclusion:** The findings demonstrate the critical value of incorporating health considerations into climate policy design and underscore the necessity of interstate cooperation to achieve equitable health benefits.

## Speaker Biography: Inês M.L. Azevedo

Inês M.L. Azevedo is an Associate Professor in the Department of Energy Resources Engineering at Stanford University. She also serves as a Senior Fellow for the Woods Institute for the Environment and a Fellow for the Precourt Institute for Energy at Stanford University.

**Research Focus:**
Prof. Azevedo's research centers on transitioning to a sustainable, low-carbon, affordable, and equitable energy system. Her work requires a systems approach, combining engineering and technology analysis with economic and decision science approaches. Her current focus is addressing energy issues with a particular emphasis on distributional effects and equity.

**Academic Contributions:**

- She has published over 100 peer-reviewed journal papers.
- She has contributed as an author and committee member to several National Research Council reports from the U.S. National Academy of Sciences.
- She serves on the editorial/advisory boards for *Environmental Research Letters*, *Annual Reviews of Environment and Resources*, *Energy Policy*, and *Energy and Climate Change*.
- She is currently participating in the IPCC AR6 as one of the lead authors for the Energy chapter.

**Awards:**

- World Economic Forum’s “Young Scientists under 40” award (2014).
- C3E Women in Clean Energy Research Award (2017).
