# Research Projects in Digital Analytics

This document summarizes two research projects focusing on advanced data analysis of user-generated content, online reviews, and digital product consumption models.

## 1. Variation in Review Writing Behavior (Social Media Analytics)

This project analyzes large datasets of online reviews to understand the external factors influencing user-generated content.

**Authors:**

- [Andreas Bayerl](https://www.eur.nl/en/people/andreas-bayerl)
- [Florian Stahl](https://www.bwl.uni-mannheim.de/quantitativemarketing/researchgroup/florianstahl/)
- Jacob Goldenberg

**Cooperation Partners:**

- IDC Israel
- Kununu.de

**Description:**
The research analyzes a dataset comprising over 10 million online reviews from two different platforms. The goal is to identify differences in review writing behavior based on external factors such as time of day, day of the week, season, weather conditions, and a city’s overall life quality.

Online reviews are often considered an unbiased source of information, but this study demonstrates how they can be significantly influenced by externalities unrelated to the reviewed instance. The methodology employs advanced techniques, including:

- Web scraping for data acquisition.
- Natural Language Processing (NLP) for data analysis.
- Machine Learning to develop models that predict review scores based on these external factors.

## 2. The Impact of Product Mix on Information Goods (Freemium Models)

This project investigates how the balance between free and paid content affects user acquisition, usage, and retention, particularly for information providers.

**Authors:**

- [Daniela Schmitt](https://www.novasbe.unl.pt/en/faculty-research/faculty/faculty-detail/id/1170/daniela-schmitt)
- Raghuram Iyengar
- [Florian Stahl](https://www.bwl.uni-mannheim.de/quantitativemarketing/researchgroup/florianstahl/)

**Cooperation Partners:**

- Wharton School (Philadelphia)

**Description:**
Information goods providers (e.g., news websites) face the challenge of balancing free users with paid subscribers. This research addresses what types of content users consume and how inter-session consumption dynamics impact their likelihood of subscribing.

Key findings suggest that increasing paid content is generally associated with higher probabilities of subscription (acquisition) and retention, moderated by the volume of user consumption. However, the study also identifies a segment of users who abandon the website when the amount of paid content increases.

**Managerial Implications:**
The results provide actionable insights for firms utilizing the freemium business model (such as streaming services or newspaper websites), detailing how changes in their product mix can impact user experience and behavior in both the short and long term.
