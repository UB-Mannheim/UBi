## Speaker Biography

**Caroline Flammer** is a Professor of International and Public Affairs and of Climate at Columbia University, with joint appointments at the School of International and Public Affairs (SIPA) and the Climate School. She also serves as a Research Associate at the National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER) and a Research Member at the European Corporate Governance Institute (ECGI).

Caroline is an expert in sustainable investing and has received numerous prestigious awards. Her research focuses on how sustainable finance and impact investing can finance a more sustainable world. Furthermore, she examines how firms can incorporate Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) considerations into their operations. This integration aims to enhance competitiveness while strengthening the system, thereby playing a critical role in addressing climate change, inequality, and global health.

Among her professional roles, Caroline serves as the President of the Alliance for Research on Corporate Sustainability (ARCS) and as the Chair of the Academic Advisory Committee of the United Nations-supported Principles for Responsible Investment (PRI). She is also an Associate Editor for *Management Science* and *The Strategic Management Journal*.

## Seminar Abstract: Financing Biodiversity

The use of private capital to finance biodiversity conservation and restoration is a developing area within sustainable finance. This study provides a conceptual framework for this practice, detailing how biodiversity can be financed through:

1. **Pure Private Capital:** Direct investment from private sources.
1. **Blended Financing Structures:** Private capital combined with public or philanthropic capital, which aims to de-risk the private investment.

The core element underlying both financing types is the "monetization" of biodiversity—the extent to which investments can generate a financial return for private investors.

**Key Findings:**

- Projects with higher expected returns tend to be financed by pure private capital, though these projects are smaller in scale and expected biodiversity impact.
- For larger-scale projects with more ambitious biodiversity impact, blended finance is the more common financing method. While these projects have lower expected returns, their risk is also lower. This suggests that blending capital is a crucial tool for improving the risk-return tradeoff and increasing appeal to private investors.
- The analysis of projects that did not reach the portfolio stage suggests that private capital requires a certain threshold in both financial return and biodiversity impact. Consequently, private capital is unlikely to replace the need for effective public policies in addressing the biodiversity crisis.
