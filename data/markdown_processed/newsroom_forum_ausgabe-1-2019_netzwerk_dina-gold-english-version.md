# Stolen Legacy: A Fight for Justice and Historical Accountability

This article details the research conducted by Dina Gold, whose book *Stolen Legacy* examines the systematic expropriation of Jewish businesses during the Nazi era. The investigation highlights the personal struggle for redress for the injustice suffered by her own family and prompts a critical examination of historical figures and institutions, including the University of Mannheim.

## The Wolff Family and Nazi Expropriation

Dina Gold's research began with the story of her great-grandfather, Victor Wolff, who owned the successful H. Wolff fur company in Berlin.

- **The Loss:** In 1937, Victoria Insurance (now part of ERGO Insurance) initiated a forced sale of the Wolff building at Krausenstraße 17/18 in East Berlin, passing it to the Reichsbahn.
- **The Struggle:** Gold traveled to Berlin after the fall of the Berlin Wall to claim compensation. The process was described as an "emotional roller coaster," requiring years of research, legal action, and family effort.
- **The Outcome:** After a long fight, the family was compensated in 1996, receiving an amount equivalent to about 19 million Euros—a rare case in German post-war history.
- **Tragedy Revealed:** During the research, the family discovered the fate of Gold's great-uncle, Fritz. While Gold's mother knew he was killed, the research revealed his name at the Sachsenhausen concentration camp in November 1938, followed by his deportation and murder at Auschwitz in 1943.

## Academic Accountability and Dr. Kurt Hamann

The research also led to a critical examination of prominent figures associated with the University of Mannheim.

- **The Discovery:** While researching, Gold discovered that Dr. Kurt Hamann, a figure long considered unproblematic and an Honorary Senator of the University of Mannheim, was the wartime CEO of Victoria Insurance.
- **The Findings:** Gold learned that Hamann was responsible for numerous "Aryanizations" during the Nazi era. Furthermore, Hamann was involved in a consortium that insured the Auschwitz, Buchenwald, and Stutthof slave labor workshops. He was also on the honorary committee of the Haus der Deutschen Kunst alongside Goebbels and Göring.
- **Institutional Response:** The University of Mannheim commissioned an academic study into Hamann’s background. Upon receiving the results, the university decided to revoke his honorary senatorial status and rename the associated foundation.
- **Current Status:** The Karlsruhe Regional Council approved changing the foundation's name to the "Foundation for the Promotion of Insurance Science at the University of Mannheim."

## The Importance of Memory and Memorialization

Gold emphasized that while the current generation is not to blame for historical atrocities, it has a responsibility to remember.

- **Advocacy:** Gold campaigned for a plaque to be attached to the Wolff building at Krausenstraße, explaining its history. She also placed a *Stolperstein* (stumbling stone) for her great-uncle Fritz in front of his former apartment on Dresdener Strasse 97 in Berlin.
- **Ongoing Effort:** The process of memorialization is continuous, requiring vigilance, as demonstrated by her recent efforts to ensure the plaque at Krausenstraße was properly cleaned and maintained.
