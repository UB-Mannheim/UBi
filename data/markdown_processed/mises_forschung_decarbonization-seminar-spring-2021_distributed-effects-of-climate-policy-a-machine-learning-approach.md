# Distributed Effects of Climate Policy: A Machine Learning Approach

**Speaker:** Christopher Knittel
**Affiliation:** Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT)

## Seminar Abstract

This seminar utilizes machine learning techniques to estimate household carbon footprints (HCFs) for the average household within each Census tract. The analysis reveals significant variation in carbon footprints based on income and geography.

**Key Findings:**

- **Income Effects:** Higher footprints are linked to transportation and consumer products and services.
- **Geographic Effects:** Variation is primarily due to the variable carbon intensity of the electricity grid.
- **Policy Assessment:** The study assesses the net effects of various climate policies on U.S. households, focusing on the distribution across geography, urbanity, and income groups.
- **Regressivity and Transfers:**
  - Regulatory standards tend to be regressive and are an average net cost to low-income households, especially in rural areas.
  - Carbon pricing, when paired with a dividend, is progressive for urban, rural, and suburban households, with low-income households receiving a larger dividend check than they spend on carbon taxes.
  - Transfers from the Midwest and Plains to the Coasts can be mitigated by slightly adjusting the dividend (an increase or decrease of less than 8%).
- **Policy Recommendations:** Policymakers are encouraged to assess and control for unwanted transfers between households, as increasing the progressive structure of a policy benefits rural households more on average and reduces the heterogeneity of impacts within income groups.

## Speaker Biography

**Christopher Knittel** is the George P. Shultz Professor of Applied Economics in the Sloan School of Management at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT). He also serves as the Director of MIT’s Center for Energy and Environmental Policy Research.

Professor Knittel joined the MIT faculty in 2011, having previously taught at UC Davis and Boston University. His research focuses on environmental economics, examining how firms and consumers respond to policy changes. He is a Research Associate at the National Bureau of Economic Economic Research.
