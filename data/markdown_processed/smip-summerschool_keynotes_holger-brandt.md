# Psychometric Complexities in Intensive Longitudinal Data

**Speaker:** Holger Brandt
**Date:** 30 July 2025
**Time:** 18:30
**Source:** [University of Mannheim - SMIP Summer School](https://www.uni-mannheim.de/smip-summerschool/keynotes/holger-brandt/)

This talk addresses the psychometric problems encountered when researchers collect intensive longitudinal data, particularly when the same items are presented repeatedly to participants.

### Key Psychometric Questions Addressed

The presentation explores several critical questions inherent in high-frequency data collection:

- How often should questionnaires be administered?
- How can researchers detect when participants become inattentive?
- What happens if an item changes its meaning for individuals who have responded to it frequently?

### Challenges in Longitudinal Measurement

The discussion focuses on psychometric issues accompanying the collection of intensive longitudinal data, such as those used in ecological momentary assessments. A major challenge is that psychometric characteristics derived from standard cross-sectional evaluations are often difficult to extrapolate to longitudinal studies involving repeated, high-frequency measures (e.g., once per day or more).

Specific issues covered include:

- **Measurement Invariance:** Heterogeneous patterns of measurement invariance that may vary across different persons and/or over time.
- **Behavioral Issues:** The occurrence of careless and insufficient effort behavior (inattention).
- **Structural Changes:** Structural changes within the dimensions that the questionnaires are intended to measure.

### Methodological Solutions

Within the framework of **Dynamic Structural Equation Modeling (DSEM)**, the talk provides ideas on how to:

1. Test for the occurrence of these complex problems within a dataset.
1. Incorporate these findings to allow researchers to extract valid conclusions from the data.

Finally, the presentation discusses the methodological challenges associated with these complex techniques, including necessary sample sizes, model fit considerations, and optimal prior tuning in Bayesian implementations.
