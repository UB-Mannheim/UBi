# CDSE Course Catalog: Spring 2022 Seminars

This catalog provides detailed information on advanced seminars and courses offered during the Spring 2022 semester, focusing on cutting-edge topics in economics, econometrics, and policy.

## 1. General Equilibrium Theory

**Prerequisites:** E700, E701, E702, E703
**Grading:** Based on final examination, problem sets, and participation in exercise sessions.

**Course Content:**
This course provides an advanced introduction to the theory of general equilibrium. It builds on introductory microeconomics (E702) and macroeconomics (E701), while utilizing mathematical tools from E700. The course treats fundamental topics including:

- Aggregate Demand
- Production Theory
- Exchange Economies
- Production Economies
- Uncertainty and Arrow-Debreu Equilibrium
- Basic Asset Pricing

The course aims to teach the fundamentals of economic equilibrium theory, laying the foundation for fields like macroeconomics, international trade, and finance.

**Competences Acquired:**
Students will master core concepts of general economic equilibrium (e.g., Walrasian equilibrium, arbitrage-free allocations, market incompleteness). They will be able to analyze problems independently across various economic fields and assess the appropriateness of equilibrium-theoretic approaches.

## 2. Dynamic Economic Modeling under Rational Expectations

**Prerequisites:** E700, E701, E702, E703
**Grading:** Based on final examination, solutions to problem sets, and participation in exercise sessions.

**Course Content:**
This module covers basic methods for dynamic economic modeling under rational expectations. Key topics include:

- Linear Rational Expectations (RE) Models: Determinacy, indeterminacy, and 'sunspot' equilibria.
- Linear RE models and Vector Auto-Regressions (VARs): State space representation, identification of economic shocks.
- Linear Quadratic (LQ) Dynamic Programming: Ricatti equation, stochastic problems, and certainty equivalence.
- Introduction to the New Keynesian Model and its Linearized Form.

**Competences Acquired:**
Students will be able to apply and understand basic tools used in business cycle analysis and follow recent literature on dynamic stochastic general equilibrium models.

## 4. Private Information and Mechanism Design

**Prerequisites:** E700, E701, E702, E703, E801, E802, E803
**Grading:** Problem sets (20%) and final exam (80%).

## 5. Heterogeneity in Macroeconomics

**Prerequisites:** E700, E701, E702, E703, E801, E802, E803
**Grading:** Written exam and assignments

**Course Content:**
This course focuses on heterogeneity in macroeconomics, introducing basic tools of modern macroeconomics and discussing various applications. Topics include:

- **Consumption/Savings:** Complete and incomplete markets, aggregate uncertainty.
- **Investment:** Neoclassical theory, adjustment costs, financial frictions.
- **Labor Markets:** Partial equilibrium search, general equilibrium search, and matching.

**Competences Acquired:**
Students gain a deep understanding of mathematical methods used in modern macroeconomics. The framework allows them to apply techniques (like dynamic programming) to problems in other fields of economics.

## 6. Advanced Econometrics: Time Series and Panel Data

**Prerequisites:** E700, E701, E702, E703, E801, E802, E803
**Grading:** Written Exam and assignments

**Course Content:**
The course covers advanced econometric techniques, including:

- Extremum estimators and M-estimation.
- Three main testing principles (LR, LM, WALD).
- Bootstrap theory applied to testing.
- Basic analysis of panel data models.
- Time series analysis (focusing on stationary setups).

**Competences Acquired:**
Students will develop strong mathematical argumentation skills and the ability to read and apply advanced scientific texts in econometrics.

**Literature:**

- Cameron, A.C. and Trivedi, P.K. (2005), *Microeconometrics: Methods and Applications*
- Wooldridge, J.M. (2010), *Econometric Analysis of Cross Section and Panel Data*

## 10. Behavioral Economics and Ambiguity

**Prerequisites:** First and second year PhD courses.
**Grading:** Presentation of Research Projects

**Course Content:**
This course addresses theoretical solutions to decision-making problems where subjective expected utility (SEU) is insufficient. It covers:

- Ellsberg’s experiments and the concept of ambiguity.
- Alternative utility measures (MEU, CEU).
- Smooth Ambiguity model and experimental design issues.

**Competences Acquired:**
Students gain the ability to understand and use non-SEU preference relations in models, test these relations experimentally, and conceptualize preference relations axiomatically.

**Schedule Highlights:**

- **Lecture/Reading Group:** 16.02.22 – 01.06.22 (Wednesday, 13:45 – 16:00)

## 11. Household Finance

**Prerequisites:** All first year CDSE or equivalent courses.
**Grading:** Research proposal (70%) and presentation (30%).

**Course Content:**
An introduction to household finance, covering the use and management of financial instruments. Topics include:

- Savings behavior (investment decisions and retirement planning).
- Credit decisions of consumers, influenced by variables like financial literacy and culture.
- Methodological discussion of information provision experiments in finance.

**Competences Acquired:**
Students learn to compare traditional and behavioral models of financial decision-making and develop research questions and experimental designs in household finance.
