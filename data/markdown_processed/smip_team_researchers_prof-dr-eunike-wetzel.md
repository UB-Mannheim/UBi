# Prof. Dr. Eunike Wetzel: Research Profile

Prof. Dr. Eunike Wetzel joined the SMiP group on April 1, 2022.

## 🔬 Research Expertise

Prof. Wetzel's research focuses on the intersection of psychometrics, personality development, and advanced measurement techniques.

- **Psychometric Modeling:** Expertise in questionnaire design and psychometric modeling, particularly Item Response Theory (IRT).
- **Personality Development:** Investigating how personality traits change across the lifespan, with a specific focus on the development of "darker" traits such as narcissism.
- **Measurement Formats:** Comparative research on different item formats, such as the Multidimensional Forced-Choice (MFC) format, to improve validity and reduce response biases (e.g., faking) compared to traditional rating scales.

### Potential Thesis Topics

Ph.D. students are encouraged to propose their own thesis topics, provided they align with Prof. Wetzel's general research interests and are compatible with the SMiP agenda (i.e., involving statistical modeling).

**1. Multidimensional Forced-Choice (MFC) Format**
The MFC format requires participants to rank items based on how well they describe them, making it less susceptible to response biases like faking than rating scales. Research has focused on comparing MFC and rating scale formats regarding validity and the process of responding to MFC triplets.

- Wetzel, E., Frick, S., & Greiff, S. (2020). The multidimensional forced-choice format as an alternative for rating scales current state of the research. *European Journal of Psychological Assessment*, 36(4), 511–515. [https://doi.org/10.1027/1015-5759/a000609](https://doi.org/10.1027/1015-5759/a000609)
- Wetzel, E., Frick, S., & Brown, A. (2021). Does multidimensional Forced-Choice Prevent Faking? Comparing the Susceptibility of the Multidimensional Forced-Choice Format and the Rating Scale Format to Faking. *Psychological Assessment*, 33(2), 156–170. [https://doi.org/10.1037/pas0000971](https://doi.org/10.1037/pas0000971)

**2. Personality Development Across the Lifespan**
While personality traits are generally stable, they do change over time (e.g., increased conscientiousness and agreeableness during young adulthood). A key area of focus is the development of "darker" traits (like narcissism) and the short-term development of personality, as most longitudinal studies have large time intervals between assessments.

- Wetzel, E., Grijalva, E., Robins, R. W., & Roberts, B. W. (2020). You're still so vain: Changes in narcissism from young adulthood to middle age. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 119(2), 479–496. [https://doi.org/10.1037/pspp0000266](https://doi.org/10.1037/pspp0000266)
