# CDSS Course Catalog Spring 2025

This catalog provides an overview of the courses offered in Computational Social Science (CDSS) for the Spring 2025 semester.

## 📚 Core Methodological Workshops (Consolidated)

### 1. Computational Social Science (CSS)

This course introduces the foundational studies, theories, and methods of Computational Social Science. CSS is an interdisciplinary field that uses large amounts of data (digital behavioral data, automated text analysis) to gain insights into society. Students learn to apply CSS methods in R, covering data management, machine learning, visualization, and statistical analysis.

- **Lecturer:** Prof. Sebastian Stier
- **Prerequisites:** Basic knowledge of statistical inference and R.
- **Assessment:** Regular small assignments (programming homework, developing research questions, and a final term paper based on an analysis in R, max. 5000 words).
- **Schedule:** Seminar, biweekly (12.02.25 – 09.04.25), Wednesday, 09:15 – 12:30 (tbc).

### 2. Advanced Quantitative Methods

This course provides a broad overview of advanced statistical techniques, including standard and advanced linear models (multiple regression, product terms, regularization), generalized linear models (logistic, Poisson, log-linear), and supervised/unsupervised classification methods (discriminant analysis, clustering, regression trees).

- **Lecturer:** N/A
- **Prerequisites:** None specified.
- **Assessment:** Participation and written exam (graded).
- **Schedule:** Seminar, 10.02.25 – 26.05.25, Monday, 10:15 – 11:45 (108 CIP Pool in B6, 30–32).

### 3. Bayesian Statistics for Social Scientists

This course introduces applied Bayesian statistics, focusing on inference based on probability theory and estimation using stochastic simulation (MCMC). Topics include linear and discrete outcome models and their hierarchical counterparts.

- **Lecturer:** N/A
- **Prerequisites:** Basic knowledge of R.
- **Assessment:** Take-home exam.
- **Schedule:** Workshop, 13.02.25 – 27.03.25, Thursday, 08:30 – 11:45 (211 in B6, 30–32).

### 4. Causal Inference in Social Science

This course tackles the problem of establishing credible causal effects in empirical analyses. Key conceptual and methodological approaches covered include the potential outcomes model, experimental designs, matching and regression, instrumental variables, regression discontinuity designs, and difference-in-differences.

- **Lecturer:** Prof. Marc Ratkovic
- **Prerequisites:** None specified.
- **Assessment:** Active participation, term paper (graded).
- **Schedule:** Workshop, 11.02.25 – 27.05.25, Tuesday, 08:30 – 10:00 (211 in B6, 30–32).

### 5. Survey Design

This graduate-level course teaches the fundamentals of survey design, covering the major steps of implementation and conducting a survey, as well as sources of error. The course uses large-scale surveys like the German General Survey (ALLBUS), European Social Survey (ESS), European Values Study (EVS), and the German Socio-economic Panel (SOEP) for illustration.

- **Assessment:** Active participation, homework assignments/oral presentations, term paper (graded).
- **Schedule:** Seminar, 13.02.25 – 22.05.25, Thursday, 13:45 – 15:15 (A103 in B6, 23–25).

### 6. Experimental Research Designs

This seminar provides a broad overview of experimental methods (survey, field, lab-in-the-field, and laboratory experiments) for causal identification. It discusses best practices, including transparency (open science) and ethical considerations.

- **Lecturer:** Dr. Sandra Morgenstern
- **Assessment:** Active participation, presentation of the Exposé of the seminar paper, research design seminar paper.
- **Schedule:** Seminar, 12.02.25 – 28.05.25, Wednesday, 13:45 – 15:15 (A103 in B6, 23–25).

### 7. Generative AI in Social Sciences

This seminar introduces selected topics relating to Generative AI in the social sciences. Students learn to apply these methods to conduct either an empirical study or a critical literature review.

- **Lecturer:** Prof. Joe Sakshaug
- **Prerequisites:** Some basic knowledge of statistical inference and R.
- **Assessment:** Term paper (5,000 words max.) on an empirical study or literature review.
- **Schedule:** Seminar, 14.02.25 – 30.05.25, Friday, 08:30 – 10:00 (Online).

### 8. Longitudinal Data Analysis

This course provides an overview of methods for analyzing panel data, including pooled OLS, fixed effects, random effects, first-differences, and event history analysis. The tutorial applies these methods using R to real survey data.

- **Lecturer:** Dr. Danielle Martin
- **Assessment:** Lecture: Three quizzes (two must be a pass), regular attendance, written examination (graded, closed-book). Tutorial: Homework and assignments.
- **Schedule:** Lecture (10.02.25 – 26.05.25, Monday, 10:15 – 11:45, B 143 in A5, 6 entrance B); Tutorial (11.02.25 – 27.05.25, Tuesday, 15:30 – 17:00, B 143 in A5, 6 entrance B).

### 9. Item Response Theory (IRT)

This workshop introduces basic and advanced models for dichotomous and polytomous items (e.g., Rasch model, partial credit model). The course includes practical exercises using R packages for modeling and analysis.

- **Prerequisites:** Basic knowledge and experience in R, including data management and use of R packages.
- **Assessment:** Presentation of IRT analysis.
- **Schedule:** Workshop (block seminar).

## 🎓 Departmental & Specialized Workshops

### 10. Dissertation Proposal Workshop (CDSS)

This workshop provides crucial feedback and support for CDSS doctoral candidates on their ongoing dissertation project. Students are expected to present their own work and provide feedback to peers.

- **Prerequisites:** Crafting Social Science Research, Literature Review.
- **Assessment:** Participation and feedback.
- **Schedule:** Workshop, 11.02.25 – 27.05.25, Tuesday, 08:30 – 10:00 (211 in B6, 30–32).

### 11. Psychology Research Colloquium

A workshop providing support and feedback for CDSS doctoral candidates in Psychology on their dissertation project.

- **Prerequisites:** CSSR, Literature Review.
- **Assessment:** Participation and feedback.
- **Schedule:** Workshop, 10.02.25 – 26.05.25, Monday, 15:30 – 17:00 (C 217 in A5, 6 entrance C).

## 🌍 Political Science & International Studies

### 12. Comparative Political Behavior

This seminar offers an overview of key concepts and theories in comparative political behavior, building on a multi-level perspective to analyze micro-level processes feeding into macro-level political processes.

- **Assessment:** Regular participation, mandatory reading, term paper (graded).
- **Schedule:** Lecture, 10.02.25 – 26.05.25, Monday, 10:15 – 11:45 (B 244 in A5, 6 entrance B).

### 13. International Political Economy

This seminar applies statistical methods to causal inference in international political economy. Topics include international migration, international organizations, and globalization, covering methods like natural experiments, DiD, and IV.

- **Assessment:** Active participation, term paper (graded).
- **Schedule:** Seminar, 13.02.25 – 22.05.25, Thursday, 12:00 – 13:30 (A301 in B6, 23–25).

### 14. Coalition Politics

This seminar addresses coalition politics throughout its life cycle, focusing on coalition bargaining, formation, and the role of agreements, especially in the context of party system fragmentation.

- **Assessment:** Active participation, oral presentation, term paper (graded).
- **Schedule:** Seminar, 12.02.25 – 28.05.25, Wednesday, 10:15 – 11:45 (C116 in A5, 6 entrance C).

### 15. UN Peacekeeping Operations

This course examines the problems and possibilities of UN peace operations, covering the evolution of mandates from ceasefires to civilian protection and human rights.

- **Lecturer:** Prof. Melanie Sauter
- **Assessment:** Term paper (max. 8000 words, graded).
- **Schedule:** Seminar, 12.02.25 – 28.05.25, Wednesday, 10:15 – 11:45 (C012 in A5, 6 entrance C).

### 16. Comparative Government and Institutions

This lecture surveys theoretical concepts and research findings in Comparative Government, focusing on the role of political institutions (e.g., electoral institutions) and their impact on political decision-making, party competition, and government formation.

- **Lecturer:** Or Tuttnauer
- **Assessment:** Written exam (graded).
- **Schedule:** Lecture, 11.02.25 – 27.05.25, Tuesday, 10:15 – 11:45 (C217 in A5, 6 entrance C).

## 🏠 Sociology & Life Course Studies

### 17. Housing and Social Inequalities over the Life Course

This seminar explores how housing intersects with social inequalities (wealth, health, education, etc.) and how these dynamics manifest across generations and social divisions.

- **Lecturer:** Malgorzata Mikucka, PhD
- **Assessment:** Participation and discussion.
- **Schedule:** Seminar, 13.02.25 – 22.05.25, Thursday, 15:30 – 17:00 (B318 in A5, 6 entrance A).
