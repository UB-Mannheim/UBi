# Colluding Against Environmental Regulation

**Speaker:** Jing Li
**Affiliation:** MIT Sloan School of Management
**Date:** November 15, 2021
**Format:** Live virtual event (Registration closed)

## Seminar Abstract

This seminar investigates collusion among firms in response to imperfectly monitored environmental regulation. Firms can improve market profits by "shading pollution" and jointly evade noncompliance penalties by "shading jointly."

The presentation quantifies the welfare effects of collusion among three German automakers regarding the reduction of diesel exhaust fluid (DEF) tanks—an emission control technology required for air pollution compliance.

**Key Findings:**

- The structural model of the European automobile industry (2007–2018) shows that while smaller DEF tanks create more pollution damages, they simultaneously improve buyer and producer surplus by freeing up valuable trunk space and reducing production costs.
- Jointly choosing small DEF tanks reduced the automakers' expected noncompliance penalties by at least 188–976 million euros.
- However, this collusion reduced overall social welfare by between 0.78 and 4.44 billion euros.

The research concludes that environmental policy design and antitrust measures play complementary roles in protecting society from collusion against regulation.

## Speaker Biography

**Jing Li** holds the William Barton Rogers Career Development Chair of Energy Economics at the MIT Sloan School of Management.

- **Research Focus:** Energy economics and industrial organization, with a focus on the development and adoption of new technologies.
- **Experience:** Served as a Postdoctoral Associate of the MIT Energy Initiative (2017–2018).
- **Recent Work:** Examines compatibility and investment in electric vehicle recharging networks in the United States, and cost pass-through in the E85 retail market.
- **Education:** Double B.Sc. degrees in Mathematics and Economics from MIT (2011); Ph.D. in Economics from Harvard (2017).
