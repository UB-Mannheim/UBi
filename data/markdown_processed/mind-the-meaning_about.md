# About the Project: Mind the Meaning

The ability to talk about ourselves is fundamental to human communication. We constantly refer to ourselves when describing our past, present, or future, and when reporting our own physiological or psychological states.

This philosophical research project investigates **self-ascriptions of mental states**—utterances such as:

- “I wish that the sun were shining.”
- “I believe that it is raining.”
- “I am in pain.”

## Core Theory: Psychological Expressivism

Specifically, we explore **Psychological Expressivism**. This theory posits that such utterances are typically *psychological expressives*. Rather than being mere assertions about the speaker and their mental states, they are understood to *express* those states.

While the basic idea of psychological expressivism has a venerable history (e.g., in Wittgenstein’s later writings), there is currently no systematic exposition of the theory.

## Project Goals and Scope

One major purpose of this project is to fill this systematic gap and embed psychological expressivism within a general theory of expressive acts.

The idea that mental states are expressed, rather than described, by avowals also promises novel insights into important philosophical debates involving first-person reference.

### Key Areas of Application

We apply the theory of psychological expressivism to several complex philosophical problems, including:

- **Self-Knowledge:** Examining the problem of self-knowledge through the expressivist lens.
- **Puzzle Cases:** Analyzing puzzle cases such as Evans’ Transparency and Wittgenstein’s two uses of “I.”
- **Classical Philosophy:** Examining the possibilities and consequences of expressivist analyses of Kant’s “I think” and Descartes’ *Cogito*.
