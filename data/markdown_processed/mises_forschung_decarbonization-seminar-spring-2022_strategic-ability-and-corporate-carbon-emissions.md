## Speaker Biography

**Sebastian Schwenen** is an Assistant Professor at the School of Management at the Technical University of Munich, where he also heads the Center for Energy Markets. His research focuses on applied microeconomics and industrial organization, with a specific emphasis on energy markets and the decarbonization of industries. Much of his recent work explores regulation and market design in low-carbon energy markets.

His research has been published in journals including the *RAND Journal of Economics*, *International Journal of Industrial Organization*, *Journal of Economics and Management Strategy*, *Review of Accounting Studies*, *The Energy Journal*, *Energy Economics*, and *Energy Policy*.

Sebastian Schwenen obtained his PhD at the Copenhagen Business School and has held visiting and research positions at prestigious institutions, including the London School of Economics and Political Science, the European University Institute, the Florence School of Regulation, and the German Institute for Economic Research (DIW Berlin). He is also a Research Affiliate at the Mannheim Center for Sustainable Energy Studies.

## Seminar Abstract

This seminar investigates the ability of firms to play oligopoly games and the resulting consequences for market efficiency and carbon emissions when firms lack strategic ability and deviate from Nash-equilibrium outcomes.

Using rich micro-level data from the Spanish electricity market, the research demonstrates that large incumbent firms tend to offer optimal output and charge optimal prices. Conversely, smaller firms often lack strategic ability and tend to "price their production out of the market."

The study shows that this heterogeneity in strategic ability significantly deteriorates the efficiency of carbon pricing because the allocation of carbon abatement across firms is not optimal. Furthermore, the findings highlight that large and strategically able firms with high shares of low-carbon generation are pivotal for achieving efficient abatement and decreasing the sector's carbon intensity. The research also computes counterfactual merger cases that suggest potential for higher carbon prices and decreased sector carbon intensity, all without incurring costs for consumers.
