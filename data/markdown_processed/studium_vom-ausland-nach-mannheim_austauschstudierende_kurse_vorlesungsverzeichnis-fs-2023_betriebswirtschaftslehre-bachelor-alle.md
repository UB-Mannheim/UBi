# Business School Course Catalog for Exchange Students

This guide outlines the available courses and academic areas for exchange students at the Business School (BWL) in Mannheim.

## 🎓 Guidance for Exchange Students

**For Bachelor's Students:**
You are advised to select courses only from the bachelor's level curriculum. Master's level courses are generally not open to undergraduate students, unless you are in your 4th year of an undergraduate program at the time of arrival in Mannheim.

**For Master's/MBA Students:**
You can choose Business courses from all available master's level courses. If your home university permits, selected bachelor's level courses are also open to you.

**Inquiries:**
For specific questions regarding course selection, please contact the Business School Exchange Team via email: [incoming.business@uni-mannheim.de](mailto:incoming.business@uni-mannheim.de).

## 📚 Academic Areas and Course Focus

### Area Accounting and Taxation

- Explain the mechanics of traditional finance and the difference between shareholder vs. stakeholder theory.
- Differentiate between ESG, socially responsible investing, and impact investing.
- Apply frameworks for measuring and evaluating social impact (e.g., logic models, SROI).
- Critically assess the moral boundaries of markets.
- Reflect on how personal values and societal norms influence financial practice.

**Key Topics Covered:**

- The Status Quo in Finance: Shareholder vs. stakeholder value; opportunity cost of capital; NPV as a decision tool; questioning whom companies serve.
- Impact Investing: Global trends, distinctions from ESG, classic approaches (microfinance, benefit corporations, UNPRI), and pitfalls in mission preservation.
- Measuring Impact: Logic models, levels of evidence, randomized control trials, SROI, and IRIS+ framework.
- Markets and Morals: Debates on commodification (e.g., organ markets, price gouging, corporate-owned life insurance); how markets can crowd out morals.
- Psychology in Finance: The role of psychology and human needs in shaping investment priorities.

### Area Information Systems

- Model complex business processes using popular modeling techniques.
- Discuss the requirements, characteristics, and effects of integrated information systems in industrial companies, including complex process interdependencies.
- Complete basic tasks from different functional areas within a wide-spread integrated information system.

**Functional Areas Covered:**

- Business Process Modelling
- Application Systems in:
  - Research and Development
  - Marketing and Sales
  - Procurement and Warehousing
  - Production
  - Shipping and Customer Service
  - Finance, Accounting, HR
- Planning and Control Systems

### Area Management

**Key Readings and Frameworks:**

- Bansal, P., & Clelland, I. (2004). Talking trash: Legitimacy, impression management, and unsystematic risk in the context of the natural environment. *Academy of Management Journal, 47*(1), 93–103.
- Hart, S.L. and Milstein, M.B. (2003). Creating sustainable value. *Academy of Management Executive, 17*(2).
- Orsato, R. (2006). Competitive Environmental Strategies: When does it pay to be green? *California Management Review, 48*(2), 127–143.
- Porter, M. E., & Kramer, M. R. (2011). Creating shared value. *Harvard Business Review, 9*(1/2), 62–77.
- Volschenk, J. (2018). The value-implications of coopetition. In *The Routledge companion to coopetition strategy*.
- The SBTi Corporate Net Zero Standard (03–2024).

**Core Themes:**

- The relationship between capitalism, wealth, and the ecological crisis.
- Investigating how business can co-create a better world through sustainable and regenerative practices.
- **Outline:**
  - Session 1: Introduction
  - Session 2: Human behaviour & policy mechanisms
  - Session 3: Business risk & corporate legitimacy
  - Session 4: Business opportunity & sustainability in strategy
  - Session 5: Environmental coopetition & value creation
  - Session 6: Group presentations

**Additional Topics:**

- Human Resource Environment
- Job Analysis & Job Design
- HR Planning & Recruiting
- Selection, Training, Performance Management, Employee Development
- Separation & Retention, Compensation, Incentives + International HRM
- High-Performance Organizations

### Area Marketing

**Learning Objectives:**

- Understand the fundamental principles involved in developing and managing marketing strategies.
- Explain the critical importance of marketing strategy for superior business performance.
- Critically evaluate a marketing strategy-related argument (e.g., whether strategy follows structure, or vice versa).
- Solve complex marketing strategy-related problems (e.g., determining the most valuable customer segments).
- Evaluate empirical and theoretical marketing strategy research from various sources.

**Key Texts:**

1. Kim, Chan W. and Renee Mauborgne (2017). *Blue Ocean Shift: Beyond Competing – Proven Steps to Inspire Confidence and Seize New Growth*.
1. Kim, Chan W. and Renee Mauborgne (2005). *Blue Ocean Strategy: How to Create Uncontested Market Space and Make the Competition Irrelevant*.
1. Palmatier, Robert W. and Shrihari Shridha (2017). *Marketing Strategy: Based on First Principles and Marketing Analytics*.
1. Rao, Vithala R. and Joel H. Steckel (1998). *Analysis for Strategic Marketing*.
1. Whitwell, Gregory, Bryan A. Lukas, and Peter Doyle (2003). *Marketing Management: A Strategic, Value-based Approach*.
