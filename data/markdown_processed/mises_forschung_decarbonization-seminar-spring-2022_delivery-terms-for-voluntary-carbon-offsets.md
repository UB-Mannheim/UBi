# Delivery Terms for Voluntary Carbon Offsets

This seminar was presented by Şafak Yücel on May 16, 2022.

## Speaker Profile

**Şafak Yücel** is an Assistant Professor of Operations Management at the McDonough School of Business, Georgetown University.

- **Research Focus:** Sustainable operations, with a specific emphasis on renewable energy. He is also interested in the economic and environmental implications of new business models.
- **Experience:** His research has been published in leading journals, including *Management Science* and *Manufacturing and Service Operations Management*. Prior to his Ph.D. in Operations Management from Duke University's Fuqua School of Business, he worked at the National Renewable Energy Laboratory of the Department of Energy.
- **Collaborators:** This work is a joint effort with Vishal Agrawal (Georgetown University) and Gokce Esenduran (Purdue University).

## Seminar Abstract: Carbon Offsets and Delivery Terms

A carbon offset is defined as one unit of reduction in greenhouse gas emissions used to compensate for emissions occurring elsewhere. Voluntary carbon offsets are purchased by companies (such as almost 40% of S&P 500 companies) to voluntarily reduce emissions, rather than for regulatory compliance. These offsets are generated by various projects, including forestry, waste disposal, and renewable energy, representing a market size exceeding $6 billion.

The paper analyzes and compares the economic and environmental implications of two primary delivery terms for voluntary carbon offsets:

### 1. Prompt Delivery

- **Mechanism:** The seller first undertakes the investment, and the yield uncertainty realizes. The seller then determines the price for carbon offsets as a recourse action.
- **Buyer Preference:** Buyers prefer prompt delivery for projects with either a low or a high investment cost, despite the seller's recourse pricing.

### 2. Forward Delivery

- **Mechanism:** A buyer orders a specific quantity of offsets *before* the seller undertakes the investment.
- **Risk:** Unlike prompt delivery, forward delivery introduces a **quantity risk**, as the seller may generate fewer offsets than the buyer's ordered quantity.
- **Buyer Preference:** Buyers prefer forward delivery for projects with an intermediate investment cost, despite the quantity risk.

**Key Finding:** The study concludes that a buyer's preference between the two delivery terms is not necessarily aligned with their environmental effectiveness, suggesting that environmental groups should exercise caution when promoting one delivery term over the other.
