### Course: Basic Mathematical Concepts (Economics)

**Prerequisites:** Basic mathematical knowledge

**Course Content:**
The course consists of four chapters:

- **Chapter 1:** Introduces and discusses basic mathematical concepts like sets, functions, and relations. Strict mathematical reasoning is explained and applied.
- **Chapter 2:** Covers the concept of metric and normed spaces, discussing the convergence of sequences in these spaces, the continuity of functions, and the concept of compact sets.
- **Chapter 3:** Deals with vector spaces, matrix algebra, linear transformation, and eigenvalues of matrices.
- **Chapter 4:** Covers a multivariate concept of differentiability and its application in solving unconstrained and constrained optimization problems.

**Competences Acquired:**
Students gain basic mathematical concepts of analysis and linear algebra. They can interpret mathematical formulas written in condensed mathematical syntax, understand the concept of a proof, and develop rigorous mathematical proofs at an elementary level. They are able to comprehend abstract mathematical concepts like metric spaces and linear spaces, and apply their knowledge, especially regarding limits, derivatives, linear algebra methods, and solving nonlinear optimization problems. Students can communicate their mathematical knowledge in English.

### Course: Microeconomic Theory (Economics)

**Prerequisites:** E700

**Assessment:**

- Written exam: 120 min (90% weighting)
- Exercises (10% weighting)

**Course Content:**
The course provides a foundation for studies in microeconomics at the Ph.D. level.

- **Part 1: Choice, preferences and utility:** Choice, preferences, and utility.
- **Part 2: Choice under uncertainty:** Expected utility.
- **Part 3:** Utility for money.
- **Part 4:** Behavioral models of choice under uncertainty.
- **Part 5 (Game Theory):** Static games of complete information (Rationalizability and iterated strict dominance).
- **Part 6:** Static games of complete information (Nash equilibrium).
- **Part 7:** Static games of incomplete information.
- **Part 8:** Dynamic games (The extensive form).
- **Part 9:** Dynamic games (Equilibrium concepts).

**Competences Acquired:**
Students acquire basic tools for graduate-level microeconomic analysis, learning how to model decision-making and strategic interactions. They gain important mathematical skills used in decision theory and game theory, enabling them to apply theory to their own research and read recent developments. The concepts serve as building blocks for advanced topics (Advanced Microeconomics II and III, macroeconomics, and empirical economics). Students also learn to write rigorous formal proofs to address microeconomic questions.

**Textbook References:**

- Fudenberg, D & Tirole, J. (1991). *Game Theory*. MIT Press.
- Kreps, D. (2012). *Microeconomic Foundation 1: Choice and Competitive Markets*. Princeton University Press.
- Mas-Colell, A. Whinston, M.D. & Green, J. (1995). *Microeconomic Theory*. Oxford University Press.
- Osborne M. and Rubinstein, A. (1994). *A Course in Game Theory*. MIT Press.

### Course: Dynamic Macroeconomics (Economics)

**Prerequisites:** E700

**Assessment:**

- Problem sets (15%)
- Midterm (90 min, 35%)
- Final exam (120 min, 50%)

**Course Content:**
This course provides an introduction to the foundations of modern macroeconomic analysis. The main object is structural dynamic models where households' preference, firms' technology, and market structure are explicitly specified. Agent behaviors are derived based on microeconomic foundations, and macroeconomic aggregates are determined by aggregating these micro-founded decisions. Applications are also considered.

**Competences Acquired:**
Students become familiar with basic methodology such as recursive methods and dynamic programming, as well as basic macroeconomic models.

**Literature:**

- Stokey, Nancy, and Robert Lucas with Edward Prescott (1989): *Recursive Methods in Economic Dynamics*. Harvard University Press.
- Ljungqvist, Lars, and Thomas J. Sargent. (2012) *Recursive macroeconomic theory*. MIT press.
- Acemoglu, Daron (2009): *Introduction to Modern Economic Growth*. Princeton University Press.

### Course: Empirical Macroeconomics (Economics)

**Prerequisites:** E700-E703, E801-E806

**Assessment:**

- *No specific weighting provided.*

**Course Content:**
This course covers methods and applications in empirical macroeconomics. Methodologically, it focuses on Structural Vector Autoregressive (SVAR) models, including various identification strategies (short-run/long-run restrictions, sign restrictions, external instruments), inference, factor models, and nonlinear models. Applications include technology, monetary, and fiscal policy shocks.

**Literature:**

- Ramey (Handbook of Macroeconomics, 2016, Volume 2A, Chapter 2: Macroeconomic Shocks and Their Propagation)
- Kilian and Lütkepohl (Structural Vector Autoregressive Analysis, 2017)
- Lütkepohl (New Introduction to Multiple Time Series Analysis, 2005)
