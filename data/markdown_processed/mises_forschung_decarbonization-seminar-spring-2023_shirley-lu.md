# Accountability of Corporate Emissions Reduction Targets

**Speaker:** Shirley Lu (Harvard University)
**Date:** May 30, 2023
**Time:** 01:45 – 03:15 PM (CEST)

**Location Details:**

- **In Person:** SO 318 (Schloss Ostflügel, Level 3, Room 18).
- **Virtual:** Advance registration required (Link no longer available).

## Seminar Abstract

Companies are increasingly announcing emissions reduction targets, but it remains unclear if firms are held accountable for them. This paper examines emissions targets that ended in 2020 to investigate which firms failed, how firms disclose the target outcomes, and if there are consequences for failing emissions targets.

Using data from the CDP, the research found that 85 firms failed to achieve their 2020 targets, representing 13% of the completed emissions targets reported for that year.

**Key Findings:**

- After a firm failed its 2020 emissions target, the study did not observe significant changes in media coverage, market response, environmental scores, or environment-related shareholder proposals.
- While setting emissions targets generates high media coverage, only four of the 85 failed firms were picked up by the media, suggesting a lack of awareness regarding target outcomes.
- The research also found evidence of reputation building, noting a positive market reaction to firms with achieved 2020 targets in subsequent target announcements.

The findings highlight the importance of institutions providing accountability for the progress of emissions targets and offer insights for targets set for 2030, 2040, and 2050.

## Speaker Biography

**Shirley Lu** is an assistant professor in the Accounting and Management Unit. She teaches the Financial Reporting and Control course in the MBA required curriculum.

Professor Lu conducts research in the area of Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) disclosure, with a focus on topics related to climate change and gender diversity. Her research aims to evaluate firm’s CSR performance, understand CSR measurements and ratings, and investigate institutional frameworks that facilitate CSR disclosures. Some of her works examine the bonding mechanisms of green bonds, and the credibility of CSR disclosures and ESG scores.

She earned a PhD in accounting and an MBA from the University of Chicago. She earned a BS in accounting and finance, and an MS in accounting, both from New York University.
