# Research Program: Political Reforms in Welfare States

The Collaborative Research Center is dedicated to gaining novel insights into the constraints on political reforms within welfare states. The interdisciplinary nature of the Center brings together over 80 senior and junior researchers from fields including economics, political science, sociology, and informatics.

## Core Research Focus and Approach

The projects hosted at the Center address the political economy of reforms through a shared, interdisciplinary approach:

- **Theoretical Evaluation:** The research evaluates competing theoretical models from a political economy perspective.
- **Empirical Testing:** The Center promotes research that empirically evaluates the extent to which various actors (citizens, interest groups, political parties, and governments) rationally evaluate reforms based on their available information, and conversely, the extent to which they fail to fully understand complex reform proposals.
- **Implications:** The findings are intended to have significant implications for both the scientific understanding of reform-making and for practical policy advice.

## Methodological Rigour and Data Infrastructure

The Center maintains a rigorous methodological approach to studying reform-making, aiming to overcome the terminological ambiguity surrounding the term "reform."

- **Bias Mitigation:** Projects are designed to avoid selection bias by analyzing not only reform failures but also both failures and successes.
- **Econometric Development:** Given the complexity of evaluating competing views on reform-making, the Center actively promotes the development of novel econometric tools for analyzing complex decisions involving multiple issues and actors with changing positions over time.
- **Data Center:** The Data Center serves as the core infrastructure, collecting and managing vast amounts of quantitative data on reform-making in Germany and other welfare states. It builds an internet panel survey to gather individual micro-data on attitudes and preferences relevant for reform decision-making, and also provides infrastructure for additional survey instruments focusing on political elites. This data is used by researchers and eventually made available to the scientific community.

## Three Research Groups

The study of political reforms requires collaborative efforts in data gathering, the evaluation of competing views, and a long-term research strategy. The Center is structured into three specialized research groups:

### Group A: Individual Attitudes and Multi-Level Aggregation of Reform Interests

This group focuses on the micro-foundations of reforms—the formation and aggregation of actors’ attitudes and interests.

- **Methodology:** Theoretical analysis primarily uses tools from game theory and mechanism design theory to study the feasibility of reforms under incomplete information regarding individual and collective costs and benefits.
- **Empirical Focus:** The theoretical analysis guides empirical research based on new data concerning individual reform preferences, reform-related knowledge, expectations, and experiments involving various policy stimuli.

### Group B: The Spatial Dimension of Reform Making in Welfare States

This area studies the spatial contexts of reforms, examining differences across countries, regions, and policy sectors.

- **Focus Areas:** Projects pay particular attention to labor markets, social security, and the public sector.
- **Analysis:** The quantitative analysis considers the contextual implications of both reform successes and failures. A special emphasis is placed on the ex-post evaluation of economic policy reforms, including Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs) in Europe and developing countries.

### Group C: The Political Process of Reform Making

This group concentrates on the political process dimension of reform-making.

- **Investigation:** Projects investigate the need for reform, the motivations, and the restrictions placed on political actors when initiating and adopting reforms.
- **Analysis:** The common empirical analysis utilizes state-of-the-art techniques to estimate the reform potential, with a particular focus on the roles of political parties.
