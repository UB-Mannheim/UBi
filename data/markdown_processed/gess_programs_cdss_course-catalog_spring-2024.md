# CDSS Course Catalog Spring 2024

This catalog provides an overview of advanced courses for doctoral candidates in the Center for Data Science and Social Science (CDSS).

## 📚 Course Offerings

### 1. Dissertation Proposal Workshop (8 ECTS)

*Applicable for Sociology, Political Science, and Psychology.*

**Course Content:**
This course provides support and crucial feedback on writing your dissertation proposal. The proposal is a research outline that must delineate your doctoral thesis project, including the motivation for research question(s), a survey of relevant theoretical and empirical contributions, the development of a theoretical framework, the specification of the methodology, and planned empirical analysis. You should be prepared to address: What makes this an interesting question? Is it an important question? What contributions would this question and the answers make to the scholarly literature? What strategies are there to answer your research question(s)?

**Prerequisites:**

- Crafting Social Science Research
- Literature Review

**Assessment:**

- Active participation

### 2. Causal Inference in Social Science

**Course Content:**
Questions of cause and effect are at the heart of social science. Establishing credible causal effects in empirical analyses is difficult. This course introduces key conceptual and methodological approaches to tackle the causal inference problem: the potential outcomes model, experimental designs, matching and regression, instrumental variables, regression discontinuity designs, and difference-in-differences and fixed effects.

**Lecturer:** Prof. Marc Ratkovic, PhD

### 8. Comparative Survey Research

**Course Content:**
Surveys are a major data source for quantitative social science research. This graduate-level course teaches the fundamentals of comparative survey research, enabling cross-national/cross-cultural analysis. The course covers the major steps of implementing and conducting a comparative survey, with a special focus on sources of error introduced by design decisions. Examples include ESS, EVS, GGS, PIAAC, and WVS.

**Prerequisites:**

- Some basic knowledge of statistical inference and R is required

**Assessment:**

### 9. Advanced Statistical Models (GLMs & Classification)

**Course Content:**
The seminar provides an overview of:

- Standard and advanced linear models (multiple regression, product terms, regularization, nonlinear regression).
- Generalized linear models (logistic regression, Poisson models, log-linear models).
- Supervised and unsupervised classification methods (discriminant analysis, clustering, regression trees, mixture models).
  Models are introduced with their mathematical and statistical foundations, illustrated with simulations and real data using R.

**Literature:**

- Dunn, P. K., & Smyth, G. K. (2018). *Generalized linear models with examples in R*.
- James, G., Witten, D., Hastie, T., & Tibshirani, R. (2013). *An introduction to statistical learning with applications in R*.

### 11. Item Response Theory (IRT)

**Course Content:**
This workshop introduces IRT with basic and advanced models for dichotomous and polytomous items. Topics include the Rasch model and extensions, partial credit/rating scale models, and multidimensional IRT models. The models are outlined with formal equations, theoretical assumptions, estimation techniques, and statistical testing procedures. Practical exercises are conducted using R packages.

**Prerequisites:**

- Basic knowledge and experience in R, including data management and use of R packages, are required.

**Assessment:**

- Presentation of IRT analysis

### 13. Natural Experiments in Political Science

**Course Content:**
Natural experiments are increasingly popular in political science research, allowing causal inferences from observational data by leveraging "as-if" random assignment. This course aims to help students recognize and discover opportunities for natural experimental research by surveying a broad range of empirical applications. Students will leave with a repertoire of potential natural experimental designs and the skills to evaluate their promises and pitfalls.

**Lecturer:** Nan Zhang, PhD

### 19. Culture and Social Learning

**Course Content:**
This course adopts a cross-species comparative perspective to understand how and why individuals (humans and nonhumans) learn in a social context—a core component of culture. It covers the mechanisms and functions of social learning, including psychological aspects (cognitive and non-cognitive) and environmental determinants.
