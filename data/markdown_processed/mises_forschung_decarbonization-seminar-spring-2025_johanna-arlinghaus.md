### Seminar Abstract

The talk investigates whether citizens support policy instruments because they appreciate their intended effects or because they are convinced by their stated objectives.

Using a large-scale representative survey with randomized video treatments, the research tests how different policy frames—specifically time savings, health, and environment—affect public attitudes towards urban tolls in two major European metropolitan areas: Berlin-Brandenburg and Paris-Ile de France.

**Key Findings:**

- Providing information on air pollution increases support by up to 11.4 percentage points (p.p.).
- Information on climate change and time savings increases support by 7.1 p.p. and 6.5 p.p., respectively.
- A causal mediation analysis demonstrates that observed changes in policy support are due to *framing effects* and do not operate via changes in beliefs about policy effects.
- The study highlights a new effect in public opinion regarding economic policies: different specified objectives for the same policy design can affect citizen views differently. Targeted information to specific population groups could significantly alter policy support.

### Speaker Biography

**Johanna Arlinghaus** is an Assistant Professor of Economics and Sustainability at the Hertie School.

She is an environmental economist specializing in fiscal and climate policy. Her methodology involves using econometric methods for causal inference and large micro-level datasets to evaluate policies.

**Experience:**

- Before joining Hertie, she was a postdoctoral researcher at Oxford University.
- She completed her PhD in Economics at TU Berlin and the Mercator Research Institute on Global Commons and Climate Change.
- She worked as an economist at the OECD Center for Tax Policy and Administration for over four years, where she designed and led projects on energy and carbon taxes and emissions trading systems in close collaboration with national policymakers.
- She holds a BSc degree in Economics from Maastricht University and an MPP from the Hertie School.
