# Financial Analytics Research Projects

This section details current research projects focusing on financial analytics, corporate finance, and market behavior.

## 1. Litigating Innovation: Evidence from Securities Class Action Lawsuits

**Authors:** [Prof. Oliver Spalt](https://www.bwl.uni-mannheim.de/spalt/), Elisabeth Kempf
**Cooperation Partner:** Chair of Financial Markets and Financial Institutions
**Description:** This study investigates how low-quality securities class action lawsuits disproportionately target firms with valuable innovation output, leading to substantial shareholder-value losses. Using data from 1996 to 2011, the research challenges the view that innovative firms are inherently riskier targets, suggesting instead that valuable innovation output makes a firm an attractive litigation target. The findings imply that the class action litigation system may negatively affect the competitiveness of the U.S. economy.

## 2. Why Does Size Matter For Bidder Announcement Returns?

**Authors:** [Prof. Oliver Spalt](https://www.bwl.uni-mannheim.de/spalt/), Christoph Schneider
**Cooperation Partner:** Chair of Financial Markets and Financial Institutions
**Description:** Analyzing US data from 1981–2014, this research examines the relationship between bidder size and bidder announcement returns (ACARs). The study finds that the sign of the coefficient on bidder size flips depending on whether the target firm is public or non-public. The authors propose an alternative model where size is not merely a proxy, but rather scales per-dollar value created (or destroyed) in a given deal, offering new insights into value creation during corporate takeovers.

## 3. Distracted Shareholders and Corporate Actions

**Authors:** [Prof. Oliver Spalt](https://www.bwl.uni-mannheim.de/spalt/), Elisabeth Kempf
**Cooperation Partner:** Chair of Financial Markets and Financial Institutions
**Description:** This project explores how investor attention influences corporate actions. By constructing firm-level shareholder "distraction" measures using exogenous shocks to unrelated parts of institutional portfolios, the research finds that firms with "distracted" shareholders are more likely to announce value-destroying acquisitions, grant opportunistic CEO stock options, cut dividends, and are less likely to fire CEOs for poor performance. These patterns suggest that unrelated shocks temporarily loosen monitoring constraints.

## 4. Mutual Fund Shareholder Letters: Flows, Performance, and Managerial Behavior

**Authors:** [Alexandra Niessen-Ruenzi](https://www.bwl.uni-mannheim.de/fakultaet/areas-und-lehrstuehle/faculty-profile/niessen-ruenzi/), [Stefan Ruenzi](https://www.bwl.uni-mannheim.de/ruenzi/team/prof-dr-stefan-ruenzi/)
**Cooperation Partner:** Chair of Corporate Governance and Chair of International Finance
**Description:** Using textual analysis, this study investigates whether the writing style of fund company shareholder letters influences fund flows, performance, and investment styles. The findings show that a less negative tone leads to higher net flows, allowing fund companies to use letters as a tactical instrument. However, the research also notes that dishonest communication inconsistent with actual performance decreases flows, and that a positive writing style predicts higher idiosyncratic risk.
