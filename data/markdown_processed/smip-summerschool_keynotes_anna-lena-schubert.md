# The Signal-to-Noise Ratio Hypothesis: A Computational Perspective on General Intelligence

**Speaker:** Anna-Lena Schubert
**Date:** 2 July 2025
**Source:** [University of Mannheim Summer School Keynote](https://www.uni-mannheim.de/smip-summerschool/keynotes/anna-lena-schubert/)

## Core Hypothesis

The talk proposes that the **Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR)** of cognitive processing serves as a global determinant of individual differences, acting as the underlying positive manifold for general intelligence ($g$).

The central argument is that a stable, system-wide SNR governs the fidelity of information processing across all cognitive operations, making it a strong candidate for explaining the general factor of intelligence.

## Computational Basis

In computational models of cognition, individual differences are typically modeled by variations in parameters. The SNR hypothesis suggests that a global characteristic like SNR would be reflected in parameters common to diverse cognitive models, including:

- Simple decisions
- Sensory discrimination
- Categorization
- Decision-making
- Learning
- Working memory

A shared feature emerges: a parameter linked to SNR, which can manifest either as variable signal strength against a fixed noise baseline, or as a noise parameter varying across individuals or tasks.

## Evidence and Methodology

The presentation will present initial evidence supporting the SNR hypothesis, focusing on:

1. **Evidence Accumulation Models:** Demonstrating correlations between SNR parameters and fluid intelligence in decision-making tasks.
1. **Working Memory Models:** Showing how SNR parameters correlate significantly with fluid intelligence.

Furthermore, the talk will introduce a **neurocognitive computational modeling approach** designed to address identifiability challenges. This framework allows for the estimation of signal and noise as distinct, interpretable parameters, offering a promising path to deepen the understanding of the cognitive and computational basis of intelligence.
