## Basic Mathematics (Fall 2022)

**Prerequisites**
Basic mathematical knowledge

**Course Content**
The course consists of four chapters:

1. **Chapter 1:** Introduces and discusses basic mathematical concepts like sets, functions, and relations. Strict mathematical reasoning is explained and applied.
1. **Chapter 2:** Covers the concept of metric and normed spaces, discussing the convergence of sequences in these spaces, the continuity of functions, and the concept of compact sets.
1. **Chapter 3:** Deals with vector spaces, matrix algebra, linear transformation, and eigenvalues of matrices.
1. **Chapter 4:** Covers a multivariate concept of differentiability and its application in solving unconstrained and constrained optimization problems.

**Competences Acquired**
Students know basic mathematical concepts of analysis and linear algebra. They can interpret mathematical formulas written in condensed mathematical syntax, understand the concept of a proof, and develop rigorous mathematical proofs at an elementary level. They understand abstract mathematical concepts like metric spaces and linear spaces and are able to comprehend argumentation based on these concepts. They are able to apply their knowledge, especially regarding the calculation of limits and derivatives, linear algebra methods, and solving nonlinear optimization problems. Students can communicate their mathematical knowledge in English.

## Game Theory (Fall 2022)

**Prerequisites**
E700

**Course Content**
The course gives a foundation for studies in microeconomics at the Ph.D. level.

- **Part 1: Choice, preferences and utility**
  1. Choice, preferences and utility
  1. Choice under uncertainty: Expected utility
  1. Utility for money
  1. Behavioral models of choice under uncertainty
- **Part 2: Game theory**
  5\. Static games of complete information: Rationalizability and iterated strict dominance
  6\. Static games of complete information: Nash equilibrium
  7\. Static games of incomplete information
  8\. Dynamic games: The extensive form
  9\. Dynamic games: Equilibrium concepts

**Competences Acquired**
Students will acquire the basic tools for graduate-level microeconomic analysis. They will learn how to model decision-making and strategic interactions. They will acquire important mathematical skills used in decision theory and game theory. They will be able to apply this theory to their own research and to read recent developments in the field. The concepts learned serve as building blocks for advanced topics (Advanced Microeconomics II and III, macroeconomics, and empirical economics). Students also learn how to write rigorous formal proofs to address microeconomic questions.

**Textbook References**

- Fudenberg, D & Tirole, J. (1991). *Game Theory*. MIT Press.
- Kreps, D. (2012). *Microeconomic Foundation 1: Choice and Competitive Markets*. Princeton University Press.
- Mas-Colell, A. Whinston, M.D. & Green, J. (1995). *Microeconomic Theory*. Oxford University Press.
- Osborne M. and Rubinstein, A. (1994). *A Course in Game Theory*. MIT Press.

## Macroeconomics (Fall 2022)

**Prerequisites**
E700

**Course Content**
This course provides an introduction to the foundations of modern macroeconomic analysis. The main object of this course will be structural dynamic models where households' preference, firms' technology, and market structure are explicitly specified. The behaviors of agents in the model economy are derived based on microeconomic foundations. The macroeconomic aggregates are then determined by aggregating individuals' micro-founded decisions. Applications will also be considered.

**Literature**

- Stokey, Nancy, and Robert Lucas with Edward Prescott (1989): *Recursive Methods in Economic Dynamics*. Harvard University Press.
- Ljungqvist, Lars, and Thomas J. Sargent. (2012) *Recursive macroeconomic theory*. MIT press.
- Acemoglu, Daron (2009): *Introduction to Modern Economic Growth*. Princeton University Press.

**Competences Acquired**
Students will be familiar with the basic methodology such as recursive methods and dynamic programming, as well as basic macroeconomic models.

## Econometrics (Fall 2022)

**Prerequisites**
E700

**Course Content**
The course offers a rigorous introduction to the foundations of modern econometric theory.

- **Part 1:** Basic notions of probability theory with their measure theoretical background (probability measure, random variables, expectations, conditional expectations, notions of convergence, and basic limit theorems).
- **Part 2:** Formal derivation of theoretical foundations of linear regression models. The theory is applied to obtain asymptotic properties of parameter estimators and to set up statistical tests.
  The module trains students in using mathematical arguments in the theory of asymptotic econometrics.

**Literature**

- Hayashi, F. (2000). *Econometrics*. Princeton University Press.
- Jacod, J. and Protter, P. (2000). *Probability Essentials*. Springer.

**Competences Acquired**
Students are expected to attain advanced theoretical knowledge in econometrics, be familiar with current theories and recent developments, and acquire a higher level of analytical capability.

## Seminar: Economic History

**Prerequisites**
All first year PhD courses

**Course Content**
This course focuses on selected topics of quantitative economic history, addressing why some countries are rich and others remain poor. Topics include trade and the growth of cities, the importance of institutions, religion, human capital, innovation, market integration, financial development, inequality, and health and epidemics. **Competences Acquired**
Students acquire thorough knowledge of empirical methods used in modern applied economics and the main topics of research in economic history. They can apply econometrics to analyze research questions in economic history and discuss potential policy implications.

## Seminar: Advanced Time Series Econometrics

**Prerequisites**
First and second year PhD courses.

**Course Content**
The lecture focuses on multivariate time series models. After reviewing non-stationary univariate models, the course covers stable VAR models (for forecasting, Granger causality, and impulse response analysis), stable VARMA processes, and integrated multivariate processes (unit root econometrics, cointegration, VEC modelling). The course addresses both asymptotic analyses and implementation issues.

**Literature**

- Hamilton, J.D. (1994). *Time Series Analysis*. Princeton University Press.
- Hayashi, F. (2000). *Econometrics*. Princeton University Press.
- Lütkepohl (2017). *Structural Vector Autoregressive Analysis*. CUP.
- Lütkepohl, H. (2005). *New Introduction to Multiple Time Series Analysis*. Springer.

**Competences Acquired**
Students acquire demanding econometric, statistical, and mathematical techniques for time series analysis. They are able to understand methodologically demanding specialist literature and conduct independent empirical analyses.
